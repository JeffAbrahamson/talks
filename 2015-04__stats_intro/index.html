Next Steps

Don't stop now! Trainings are most useful if you apply what you've learned within the few weeks following the course. Think of ways to use statistical significance, regression, and classification algorithms in your daily life, even if the uses aren't perfect. The immediate goal is to get yourself to use this knowledge so it becomes a part of you.

One path we mentioned at the end of Wednesday is the Kaggle Titanic challenge. It's a great introductory-level, but very real, challenge. In addition, the site includes a number of great getting started tutorials, notably this video.

Here are some additional reading suggestions to help keep you going:

  • Useful Things to Know about ML, a great article by Pedro Domingos with tips on the black art of using machine learning effectively.
  • Advice from Andrew Ng on applying ML.
  • We used pandas in the spam classification exercise. The web has many good introductions to pandas. Once you understand pandas a bit, I highly recommend Greg Reda's introduction to working with DataFrames. (The page includes a link to his conference talk, if you'd rather ingest the information by video.) A very complete introduction is this book.
  • Radim Rehurek's talk on text ML is also quite good. He presents a slightly different approach to spam filtering than we covered in class.

In addition, pyvideo.org is a great ressource for python and python tools learning. Here are a couple videos particularly worth watching:

  • IPython in depth
  • Exploring Scikit-learn
  • Diving deeper into scikit-learn
  • Skipper Seabold's Statsmodel tutorial.
